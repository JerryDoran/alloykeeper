---
title: PWA-1480
description: PWA 1480 is a first-generation single-crystal nickel-based superalloy developed by Pratt & Whitney for use in high-temperature gas turbine applications. PWA 1480 is a precipitation-hardened, single-crystal superalloy designed for excellent high-temperature strength and creep resistance. PWA 1480 was one of the first single-crystal superalloys to be widely used in commercial and military jet engines. Its development in the late 1970s marked a significant advancement in turbine blade technology, enabling higher operating temperatures and improved engine efficiency. PWA 1480's combination of high-temperature strength, creep resistance, and single-crystal structure made it a pioneering material in the evolution of gas turbine technology, paving the way for subsequent generations of single-crystal superalloys.

## Properties

- γ (gamma) matrix with coherent γ' (gamma prime) precipitates.
- Absence of grain boundaries due to single-crystal structure.
- Carefully controlled distribution of γ' precipitates for optimal strength.
- Density: ~8.7 g/cm³.
- Excellent oxidation resistance.
- Good hot corrosion resistance.
- Melting Range: ~1315-1335°C.
- Thermal Expansion Coefficient: ~14 x 10^-6 /°C (20-1000°C).
- Thermal Conductivity: ~8-10 W/m·K.
- Service Temperature: Up to 1050°C with appropriate coatings

**Mechanical properties:**

| Property         | Value(Room Temp)               |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Tensile Strength | 1,000–1,200 MPa                |
| Yield Strength   | 900–1,000 MPa                  |
| Elongation       | ~10%                           |
| Creep Rupture    | 137 MPa at 1,000 hours @ 982°C |

**Advantages**

- High strength at elevated temperatures.
- Excellent creep resistance.
- Superior fatigue resistance due to single-crystal structure.
- Good oxidation and hot corrosion resistance.
- Thermal stability for long-term service.

**Limitations**

- Complex and costly manufacturing process.
- Limited weldability.
- Anisotropic properties due to single-crystal structure.
